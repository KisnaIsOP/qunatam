Fundamental Concepts

Wave-Particle Duality

Wave-particle duality is a fundamental principle of quantum mechanics that describes how every particle or quantum entity can be described as either a wave or a particle. Key points:

  • Particles like electrons and photons can behave as both waves and particles
  • The wave nature is described by the wave function
  • Measurement causes the wave function to collapse into a particle state

Real-World Example:

Light behaving as both a wave (interference in double-slit experiment) and as particles (photoelectric effect)

Quantum Superposition

Quantum superposition states that particles can exist in multiple states simultaneously until measured. Understanding superposition:

  • A quantum system can exist in multiple states at once
  • Measurement forces the system to choose one state
  • Forms the basis for quantum computing

Famous Example:

Schrödinger's cat thought experiment - a cat that is simultaneously alive and dead

Quantum Entanglement

Quantum entanglement occurs when particles become correlated in such a way that the quantum state of each particle cannot be described independently. Key aspects:

  • Einstein called it "spooky action at a distance"
  • Measuring one particle instantly affects its entangled partner
  • Used in quantum cryptography and quantum computing

Application:

Quantum cryptography uses entanglement for secure communication

Mathematical Foundation

Wave Functions

The wave function (Ψ) describes the quantum state of an isolated quantum system:

Ψ(x,t) = Ae^(i(kx-ωt))

Where:

  • A = amplitude
  • k = wave number
  • ω = angular frequency

Schrödinger Equation

The fundamental equation of quantum mechanics:

iℏ∂Ψ/∂t = ĤΨ

Components:

  • ℏ = reduced Planck's constant
  • Ĥ = Hamiltonian operator
  • Ψ = wave function
